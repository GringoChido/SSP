Bassist / Composer / Conductor

Derrick Hodge

Philadelphia, PA Film & Orchestral Composer Cultural Force

Derrick Hodge operates at a scale most musicians never attempt — bassist, composer, conductor, producer, and cultural institution builder, all at once and without compromise. The Philadelphia native has quietly assembled one of the most remarkable résumés in modern American music, serving as the connective tissue between jazz, hip-hop, classical, and cinema in ways that have made history more than once.

Hodge directed the music for the 2022 Academy Awards and arranged for Nas at the Grammys. He conducted the first all-Black orchestra at the Hollywood Bowl for CNN’s Juneteenth concert. He became the first Black composer to write hip-hop for the National Symphony Orchestra, staging the twentieth-anniversary celebration of Nas’s Illmatic at the Kennedy Center. He was the first to compose strings for hip-hop at Carnegie Hall and the first to write symphonic hip-hop with the Houston Symphony. The Smithsonian’s National Museum of African American History and Culture commissioned him to create music for its permanent exhibitions. Each of these milestones would define a career; Hodge treats them as chapters.

His solo work on Blue Note Records — Live Today, The Second, and Color of Noize — reveals a composer who moves between intimate acoustic meditation and orchestral grandeur with equal authority. As a founding member of Robert Glasper Experiment, R+R=Now, and the Blue Note All Stars, he has shaped some of the most important ensemble recordings of the past decade. His decade-long tenure as Maxwell’s musical director and production work alongside Quincy Jones and Don Was further cement his range.

Hodge scored Stella Meghie’s film The Photograph and debuted his Color of Noize Orchestra at the Hollywood Bowl, expanding his vision from the rhythm section to the conductor’s podium. He is, by any measure, one of contemporary music’s most complete and consequential artists — a figure whose work reshapes not just how music sounds but where and for whom it is performed.
